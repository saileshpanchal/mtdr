# Customer Outcomes

The assurance question is not "does this comply" but "who receives a worse outcome, even when
everything works as designed". Read `spec-decision-assurance.md` §5 for where the output lands.

## The one rule

**Controls are product decisions too.** Every protective measure is experienced by far more
legitimate customers than abusers — assure it as the product feature it is.

## Process

1. **Name the cohorts, not the average.** Target market; excluded cohorts; customers with
   characteristics of vulnerability; the digitally excluded; those using assistive technology or
   intermediaries. Averages conceal the harm — differential outcomes between cohorts are the
   finding.
2. **Project the decision as designed.** Even working perfectly: who pays more, waits longer,
   understands less, or loses access? Financial and non-financial harm both count.
3. **Project every control** (take the list from the adversarial pass): false-positive denials;
   lost account or payment access; friction and abandonment; unequal effects across cohorts;
   delayed resolution and redress; reduced understanding or agency. A control that shifts harm
   onto the customers least able to absorb it has not reduced harm — it has moved it.
4. **Check price and value.** Does the decision or its controls change what any cohort pays
   relative to what they receive — including in time, effort and risk carried?
5. **Check understanding and support.** Can affected customers understand what changed, contest a
   wrong outcome, and reach redress — through the channels *they* actually use?
6. **Trace the distribution chain.** Where intermediaries, platforms or agents sit between the
   organisation and the customer, whose incentives change and what reaches the end customer.
7. **Convert to hypotheses.** Every projected harm becomes a monitoring-contract entry with a
   cohort-differentiated metric and threshold. Process-completion measures ("we reviewed it") are
   not outcome measures.

## Regulatory worked example — not a dependency

The UK's **Consumer Duty** expects firms to avoid foreseeable harm and to assess, test, understand
and evidence customer outcomes — with fair-value analysis reflecting the assessment made *when the
decision is taken*, and monitoring that distinguishes between customer groups. It also treats
inadequate fraud protection and inadequate victim support as foreseeable harm. This skill produces
exactly that evidence, at decision time; other jurisdictions will recognise the same test.

## Anti-patterns

- **The compliance lag** — running outcomes as a separate checklist after the decision is made.
- **Average-customer assurance** — testing the journey on the cohort it was designed for.
- **Protection blindness** — assessing the fraud saved and never the customers locked out.
- **Process-completion metrics** — evidencing that reviews happened rather than what customers received.
