# TDR boundary FAQ

Short answers to the boundary questions that come up when the TDR meets an existing records estate.

## Is a TDR a consent receipt, an identity claim, a runtime audit log or a model trace?

No. Those records prove permission, identity, execution or behaviour. A TDR records the organisational judgement that authorised the action, selected the limits, accepted the risk, assigned accountability and defined the evidence required to prove the decision remained valid in operation.

The four adjacent records each answer a different question:

| Record | Proves |
|---|---|
| **Consent receipt** | Permission — that a party agreed to the action |
| **Identity / federation assertion** | Who is acting |
| **Runtime log / model trace** | What happened — execution and behaviour |
| **TDR** | Why the organisation believed the action was legitimate |

## Does a TDR replace any of them?

No. It sits upstream of all four and cites them as evidence. A consent receipt proves a customer agreed; it does not prove anyone judged the ask to be fair. A runtime log proves what a system did; it does not prove anyone decided it should. When a reviewer works backwards from an event, the operational records answer *what happened* — the TDR is the record that answers *on whose judgement, within what limits, and why that was believed legitimate*.

## So when something goes wrong, which record answers?

All of them, in order. The log establishes what happened; the identity assertion establishes who acted; the consent receipt establishes what was permitted. The question that remains — *was the organisation entitled to be doing this at all, and who accepted the risk?* — is the one only a decision record can answer, and it is the one a board, auditor or supervisor asks last and hardest.
